“A brief history of American space exploration”

The aftermath of World War II left a power vacuum that the Soviet Union sought to fill. According to Joseph C. Grew “a future war with Soviet Russia is as certain as anything in this world can be certain” (Challener, 1953, p. 279). The USSR’s aggression in Eastern Europe prompted the United States to adopt a policy of containment, setting the stage for the Cold War (Wilde, 2019). Characterized by little direct fighting between the US and USSR, this period primarily consisted of proxy wars, displays of power, and technological ability, such as the nuclear arms race and space race.

As the Cold War unfolded, the US and USSR vigorously competed to establish their respective economic systems, capitalism and communism, as dominant in the rapidly evolving world. The space race, a key aspect of this competition, built upon the advances made in rocketry by both countries’ nuclear weapon programs. Unlike other efforts during the Cold War, the space race was not primarily driven by the goal of creating a military threat to the enemy but rather aimed to showcase technological capabilities and create a psychological impact on the opponent (American Observer, 1961).

The nation that demonstrated the most advanced spaceflight capabilities would accumulate the most international prestige and increased support at home. Throughout the space race, the US and USSR traded blows, with both reaching spectacular accomplishments (History.com, 2010). The US famously landed men on the moon, while the USSR achieved early successes with Sputnik, the world’s first artificial satellite, and the first human spaceflight.

As we explore the early days of American space exploration, this blog series will take you on a journey from the pioneering days of artificial satellites to more recent missions that continue to push the boundaries of what’s possible. In the next post, we’ll review the defining programs of the American space program during this period, ranging from the Mercury Program to the Apollo missions that put men on the moon (NASA, n.d.). We’ll explore the successes and setbacks, as well as the technological advancements and human achievements that made America’s space program a global leader.
