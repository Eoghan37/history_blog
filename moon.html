“Men on the Moon”

After Russia’s first successful manned mission, then US President John F. Kennedy began to rally support the US space program, stating that the US needed to embark on space missions grand enough to surpass the USSR (Kennedy, 1962). Both a crewed moon landing and a space station were considered to achieve this goal, and the moon landing was chosen as the priority (Braun, 1962). Through a series of speeches, Kennedy justified it under the principles of national security and scientific advancement.

As the moon program began to take shape, NASA turned its attention to the simpler task of crewed spaceflight. The Gemini project, consisting of 10 manned missions, demonstrated incremental capabilities with each successive flight. The program developed a two-person spacecraft and pioneered solutions to many unsolved challenges, such as docking and performing extravehicular activities.

The Gemini project’s advances facilitated the Apollo missions, which would ultimately take humans to the moon, (NASA, n.d.). However, tragedy struck on January 27, 1967, during a ground test in preparation for the planned February launch. An electrical fire took the lives of all three onboard crew members, a painful reminder of the importance of rigorous safety protocols and design (NASA, 1967). In response, NASA took action solve all known safety risks, and underwent an organizational restructuring in an attempt to prevent any such mistakes from ever occurring in the future.

The Soviet moon landing program also faced tragedy during Soyuz 1, which encountered a series of technological problems, leading to a mission abort that caused the loss of its crew due to a parachute failure (BBC, 1967). Despite these setbacks, both programs ultimately recovered, driven by their shared determination to reach the moon.

In late 1967, the US accomplished a successful uncrewed launch of its Saturn V rocket (Cadbury, 2006, pg. 310-316), while the Soviets’ next piloted mission, Soyuz 3, executed without control or recovery problems (Hall, 2003, pg. 144-147). NASA’s Apollo Program was in the lead, demonstrating rapid achievement through an uncrewed and later a crewed orbit of the moon.

On July 20, 1969, as part of Apollo 11, the United States successfully landed the first humans on the moon, marking a monumental triumph in American space exploration history (NASA, 1969). During this same period, the Soviet space program faced many setbacks due to reliability and other concerns, ultimately never landing a man on the moon.
