“Life in the Atmosphere”

The dawn of the American space program began with an ambitious goal: to launch the first American into space. This historic milestone the Mercury Program sought to accomplish marked the beginning of a new era in space exploration.

In 1959, the Mercury Program was born, with the mission to launch a manned spacecraft into orbit around the Earth. This bold initiative was originally conceived as “Man in Space Soonest,” a project from the US Air Force, and was subsequently transferred to NASA. The program’s primary objective was to demonstrate the capability to launch a human into space, a prerequisite to any future human spaceflight endeavors (NASA, 1998, pg. 75).

The innovative design of the Mercury spacecraft was a marvel of its time. It was a conical capsule with a cylindrical pack of retro-rockets and a heat shield on the blunt end and, through extensive testing, it proved a safe and reliable ride for the astronauts (Catchpole, 2001).

The selection of the Mercury Seven astronauts was a rigorous process for even the most skilled test pilots. The seven chosen astronauts – Alan Shepard, Gus Grissom, John Glenn, Scott Carpenter, Wally Schirra, Gordon Cooper, and Deke Slayton – demonstrated exceptional flying skills and the ability to withstand the extreme conditions of space travel.

After a series of weather-related delays, the Mercury-Redstone 3 mission was ready to launch. On May 5, 1961, Alan Shepard became the first American in space, exercising manual control over the spacecraft’s attitude and propulsion throughout a ballistic trajectory (NASA, 1998, pg. 346-364). This historic flight marked a turning point for American space exploration, demonstrating the country’s capabilities in spaceflight and validating the technology that would facilitate their future ambitions.

The subsequent Mercury flights built upon Shepard’s success. Gus Grissom repeated Shepard’s suborbital flight on July 21, 1961, in Liberty Bell 7, and John Glenn became the first American to orbit the Earth on February 20, 1962, in Friendship 7 (NASA, 1998, pg. 424-437). These flights allowed America to perfect basic space, and solidified the Mercury Program’s significance in American space exploration history.

The Mercury Program marked the beginning of human spaceflight in the United States, setting the stage for future space exploration milestones. The program’s success demonstrated the country’s capabilities, facilitating future achievements and inspiring the world.
